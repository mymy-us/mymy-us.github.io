The Two Halves of Your Lean Job

By Tim Y |Posted on Sep 7, 2025 | 3 min read

Topic: The Reality of a Lean Engineer’s Role
The Trap: Thinking your job is just about fixing things—finding and solving another then another problem problem.

If you ask a professor what a Lean Engineer does, they’ll probably say something only focus on continuous mprovement.
But If you ask me, I’ll say the job actually contains two distinct battles:

  1. The Attack: Continuously Eliminating Waste.
  2. The Defense: Sustaining Gains.

Phase 1: The Attack

You are a detective, trying to find the glitch, and its solution.

In this phase, you can be a Maverick. You can run the data, identify the bottleneck, and design a fix all by yourself. You are a hero in your head.

The result: You come up with a proposal/solution that could save the company lots of money. You feel like a genius.

Phase 2: The Defense

This is where a typical introvert engineer feels hard.

You have improved the process by testing with your own hands and taught the line leader and operators on site. Now, how can you be sure that the operators don’t go back to the old way the second you leave the jobshop?

The Paper Shield is Weak
In school, you are taught to update the documentation:

  • FMEA
  • Control Plans
  • Standard Operating Procedures
  • Standard Inspection Procedures
  • Parameter Checksheet
  • Publishing via the Product Lifecycle Management system

The Reality: Documents are very passive. They live on servers. And the operators on site basically earn their paychecks by providing manual labor, not by heavily using their brain. So, most of the time, people on site don’t care about those lengthy papers at all, unless it comes with punishment. But such practice is highly disencouraged.

To sustain change, you need Active Enforcement:

  1. Visual Management: Don’t just write a rule; put tape on the floor or a shadow board on the wall. Make it impossible to ignore.
  2. Layered Process Audits: If management doesn’t check it, operators won’t do it.
  3. Gemba Walks: You need to be seen on the floor, verifying the fix.

But even with these tools, you need Influence.

The Owner Mindset

To survive Phase 2, you need to drop the “I’m just a new grad” attitude.

You need to walk into the Kaizen project with absolute, unwavering confidence. You need the attitude of:

“I am the subject matter expert. Nobody knows this better than me.”

It sounds arrogant? Good, and that is how an excellent engineer survives and leads in a corporate. If you are leading a change, you are the de facto leader in that room.

  • Don’t sit in the back corner of the meeting.
  • Don’t be afraid of the production manager.
  • Do speak first. Frame the problem. Own the solution.
  • Do actively seek helps from your own department.

How to Win the Turf War

You cannot order people from other departments to listen to you. You have no authority over them in corporate structure.

You have to find the correct person to help that happen.

  1. Find the Department Head: The person who actually owns the staff you are trying to change.
  2. Sell the Benefit: Don’t say “It’s Lean.” Tell them, “This change will save your team 20 hours of overtime cost every month.”
  3. Get the Endorsement: Once the department head sees the benefit, ask them to announce it.

When the head says, “We are doing this,” the team follows. You are just the architect; let the department head be the enforcer.

The Takeaway

Phase 1 (Fixing) requires IQ.
Phase 2 (Sustaining) requires EQ and Guts.

Don’t just be a smart engineer. Be a loud one too.
