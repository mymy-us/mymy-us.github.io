The Two Halves of Your Lean Job (And Why the Second Half is Harder)

Topic: The Reality of a Lean Engineer’s Role
The Trap: Thinking your job is just “fixing things.”

If you ask a professor what a Lean Engineer does, they’ll say: “Continuous Improvement.”
If you ask me, I’ll tell you the job is actually two distinct battles:

  1. The Attack: Continuously Eliminating Waste.
  2. The Defense: Sustaining the Gains.

Most new grads crush the first part and fail the second. Here is why.

Phase 1: The Maverick (The Attack)

This is the fun part. You are the detective.

You walk onto the floor, clipboard in hand, looking for Shingo’s 7 Wastes (Defects, Overproduction, Waiting, etc.) plus the deadliest one: The Waste of Human Talent/Ideas.

In this phase, you can be a Maverick. You can run the data, identify the bottleneck, and design a fix all by yourself. You are the hero with the spreadsheet.

The result: You save the company money. You feel like a genius.

Phase 2: The Politician (The Defense)

This is where the “Introvert Tax” hits hard.

You improved the process. Great. Now, how do you make sure the operators don’t go back to the old way the second you leave the room?

The “Paper Shield” is Weak
In school, they teach you to update the documentation:

  • Control Plans
  • Standard Operating Procedures (SOPs)
  • Publishing via the PLM (Product Lifecycle Management) system

The Reality: Documents are passive. They live on servers. To sustain change, you need Active Enforcement:

  1. Visual Management: Don’t just write a rule; put tape on the floor or a shadow board on the wall. Make it impossible to ignore.
  2. LPA (Layered Process Audits): If management doesn’t check it, operators won’t do it.
  3. Gemba Walks: You need to be seen on the floor, verifying the fix.

But even with these tools, you need Influence.

The “Owner” Mindset

To survive Phase 2, you need to drop the “I’m just a new grad” attitude.

You need to walk into the Kaizen project with absolute, unwavering confidence. You need the attitude of:

“I am the Subject Matter Expert. Nobody knows this data better than me.”

It sounds arrogant? Good.
If you are leading a change, you are the de facto leader in that room.

  • Don’t sit in the back corner of the meeting.
  • Don’t be afraid of the Production Manager.
  • Do speak first. Frame the problem. Own the solution.
  • Do actively seek helps from your own department.

How to Win the “Turf War”

You cannot order people from other departments to listen to you. You have no authority over them.

You have to use “The Borrowed Sword.”

  1. Find the Department Head: The person who actually owns the staff you are trying to change.
  2. Sell the Benefit (WIIFM): Don’t tell them “It’s Lean.” Tell them, “This change will save your team 20 hours of overtime a month.”
  3. Get the Endorsement: Once the Department Head sees the benefit, ask them to announce it.

When the boss says, “We are doing this,” the team follows. You are just the architect; let the Department Head be the enforcer.

The Takeaway

Phase 1 (Fixing) requires IQ.
Phase 2 (Sustaining) requires EQ (Emotional Intelligence) and Guts.

Don’t just be a smart engineer. Be a loud one.
