    How to Ask 5WHYs Correctly

    By Tim Y |Posted on Oct 22, 2025 | 3 min read

    Topic: Root Cause Analysis & The 5 Whys
    The Academic View: To find a root cause, sit down and ask yourself “Why?” five times until you reach the core problem.
    The Reality: If you ask the wrong “Why,” the direction will deviate and you will probably end up blaming human error or bad management. You cannot engineer a fix for either of those.

    The biggest mistake novices make with the 5 Whys is focusing on the answer. But the real challenge is actually knowing how to ask a better question.

    A Novice’s 5 Whys

    Let’s look at a classic example of a bad 5 Whys analysis:

    • Problem: The car broke down on the highway.
    • Why 1: Why the car broke on the middle of a road?—Because the engine failed.
    • Why 2: Why the engine failed?—Because the engine oil went bad.
    • Why 3: Why the engine oil went bad?—Because I didn’t change the oil in time.
    • Why 4: Why did you forget to get an oil change?—Because I forgot to check the mileage.
    • Why 5: Why did you forget to check the mileage?—Because of poor personal planning (management/human error).

    The Result: The corrective action is “Try harder to remember me next time” or “Retrain the driver.”

    In the manufacturing world, all engineers share the view that manual work is always unreliable and prone to errors; so, this is pretty much a useless fix. If you submit a report like this to a customer that says “Operator forgot,” they will reject it and note you as incompetent. If your root cause relies on a human “trying harder,” the defect will definitely happen again, no matter how hard he/she tries.

    How to Ask Better Questions

    To do a high-quality 5 Whys analysis, you need to change how and where you ask the questions:

    Rule 1: Ban the words “Forgot,” “Careless,” and “Management.” If an operator forgot a step, do not ask “Why did they forget?” That always leads to psychology and objection. Instead, ask: “Why did the production system allow the following process to continue even after a step was completely missed?” And that leads to engineering.

    Rule 2: Focus on Physics and Process, not Psychology. Always direct the questions back to the physical mechanics of the workstation, the wear on the tooling, or the rigid logic of the system.

    Rule 3: Split the Problem in Views of Occurrence vs. Escape You can run two separate tracks of Whys:

    1. Occurrence: Why did the machine make the bad part?
    2. Escape: Why did our inspection fail to catch it before it shipped?

    Rule 4: Never do it at your desk. You cannot ask “Why?” to a white board. You must go to the gemba—the factory floor. Look at the broken part, watch the machine cycle, and ask the operator. If you keep guessing the answers in a conference room and are too busy or too introvert to walk on the field, you will fix the wrong problem.

    The Professional 5 Whys

    Let’s re-do the car example using the mindset.

    • Problem: The car broke down from engine failure.
    • Why 1: Why the car broke in the middle of a road?—The oil lost its viscosity and failed to lubricate the cylinders. (Physics)
    • Why 2: Why the oil lost its viscosity?—The oil remained in the car 5,000 miles past its operational limit. (Process)
    • Why 3: Why the car could still drive after passing the mile limit by so much?—The driver was not notified that the limit was reached. (System)
    • Why 4: Why there was no notification?—The car does not have a sensor to track mileage and alert the user automatically. (Design)

    The Result: The corrective action is to install a maintenance light on the dashboard that triggers to alert the driver.

    The Takeaway

    If your 5 Whys end with management problem or operator error, you have stopped too early. People are flawed. They will always forget, get tired, and make mistakes. So don’t blame people, focus on process and physics.
