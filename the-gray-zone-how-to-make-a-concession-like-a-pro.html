The Gray Zone: How to Make a Concession Like a Pro

By Tim Y |Posted on September 24, 2025 | 2 min read

Topic: Quality Judgments & Concessions
The Academic View: Specifications are black and white. Good parts ship. Bad parts are scrapped.
The Reality: Manufacturing lives in the gray zone. If you scrap every part with a microscopic anomaly, your company will go bankrupt.

Sometimes, you have to make a Concession (accepting a part that deviates slightly from the perfect standard). Here is how to make that judgment call without risking your job.

1. The Golden Rule: Form, Fit, and Function

When you find a deviation, ask one question: Does this affect Form, Fit, or Function (FFF)?

  • Internal Concession: If the defect is strictly cosmetic (e.g., a scratch on a surface hidden inside the machine), it does not affect FFF. You can document the deviation, approve it internally, and ship it. You save the company money.
  • External Concession: If the deviation changes how the part fits or works, you must ask the customer for a formal waiver. Never hide a functional defect. That is a breach of contract.

2. The “Undefined” Defect (Creating the Boundary)

What happens when you find a weird visual defect that isn’t even listed in your SIP? You have to create the standard on the spot.

  1. Find the absolute worst-case part that you still believe is acceptable.
  2. This physical part becomes your Boundary Sample (Limit Sample).
  3. Sign it, date it, and place it at the inspection station. Now the operators aren’t guessing; they are comparing.

3. Judging the “Borderline” (The Batch Context)

When dealing with a minor cosmetic issue, you have to judge the context of the whole batch. Customers usually accept a tiny percentage of minor cosmetic flaws (called an Acceptable Quality Limit, or AQL).

  • Healthy Batch: If the rest of the batch is running perfectly, you can afford to be slightly lenient and internally concede a few borderline cosmetic parts.
  • Sick Batch: If the process is unstable and the batch is already close to the rejection limit, your buffer is gone. Every borderline part must be strictly scrapped to protect the shipment.

4. The “Complaint Tax” (Dynamic Standards)

Here is the secret they don’t teach in school: The passing score is dynamic.

Let’s say your standard inspection strictness is a 90/100. If the customer called you yesterday screaming about a quality issue, your new passing score is 95/100.

  • Why? Because the customer is currently hyper-sensitive. They will inspect your next shipment with a magnifying glass.
  • A borderline defect that you safely approved last month is an immediate “Scrap” this month. You must temporarily over-inspect to rebuild trust.

The Takeaway

Making a concession is not “cheating.” It is calculated risk management.

  1. Protect Form, Fit, and Function above all else.
  2. Let the context of the batch dictate how you handle the gray area.
  3. Adjust your strictness based on the customer’s current trust level.
