    How to Make a Concession Like a Pro

    By Tim Y |Posted on Sep 24, 2025 | 3 min read

    Topic: Quality Judgments & Concessions
    The Academic View: Specifications are black and white. Good parts ship. Bad parts are scrapped. Nobody can change nothing.
    The Reality: Manufacturing lives in the gray zone. If you scrap every part with a microscopic anomaly, the profit margin of the product would be eaten up easily.

    Sometimes, you have to make a concession (accepting a part that deviates slightly from the perfect standard, as said in ISO9001), balance the cost and risk like a pro.

    1. The Golden Rule: Form, Fit, and Function

    When you find a deviation, ask yourself one question: Does this affect Form, Fit, or Function (FFF) of the product?

    • Internal Concession: If the defect is strictly cosmetic (e.g., a scratch on a surface), it does not affect FFF. You can document the deviation, approve it internally, and ship it and you have saved the company some money like a senior engineer, who knows how to protect the business. But remember, sometime cosmetic is the fuction, if a part’s face is used at the outer side of a final product and final customers can see it easily.
    • External Concession: If the deviation changes how the part fits or works, you must ask the client for a formal waiver. Never hide a functional defect. That is a breach of contract.

    2. The Undefined Defect

    What happens when you find a weird defect that isn’t even listed in your SIP or could be found in PPAP documents? You have to solve it and create the standard on the spot for the parts that has been produced.

    1. Find the absolute worst-case part that you still believe is acceptable.
    2. This physical part becomes your boundary sample, or limit sample.
    3. Sign it, date it, and place it at the inspection station. Now the operators aren’t guessing; they are comparing.

    No matter what, an internal report to your superior is always required and knowledge sharing between your colleagues is in favor.

    However, it is a little bit tricky on communication strategy to the client about this matter. If the undefined defect affects the FFF, or the related term has a higher FMEA score, you should always ask the client for a formal approval. And you always ought to prepare the client rejecting your request without a reason or doing you a favor, simply saying: “The PSW has signed and PPAP has frozen. It is your problem.”

    3. Judging the Borderline in a Batch Context

    When dealing with a minor cosmetic issue, you have to judge the context of the whole batch. Clients usually accept a tiny percentage of minor cosmetic flaws (called an Acceptable Quality Limit, or AQL).

    • Healthy Batch: If the rest of the batch is running perfectly, you can afford to be slightly lenient and internally concede a few borderline cosmetic parts.
    • Sick Batch: If the process is unstable and the batch is already close to the rejection limit, your buffer is gone. Every borderline part must be strictly scrapped to protect the shipment.

    4. The Complaint Tax Dynamic Standards

    Here is the secret they don’t teach in school: The “passing score” is dynamic.

    Let’s say your standard inspection strictness is a 90/100 in a mathmatical view. If the client called you yesterday screaming about a quality issue, your new passing score has definitely become higher, like to 95/100 for example.

    • Why? Because the client is currently hyper-sensitive. They will inspect your next shipment with a magnifying glass.
    • A borderline defect that made your last month in peace is an immediate “Scrap” this month. You must temporarily over-inspect to rebuild trust.

    The Takeaway

    Making a concession is not cheating. It is calculated risk management.

    1. Protect Form, Fit, and Function above all else.
    2. Let the context of the batch dictate how you handle the gray area.
    3. Adjust your strictness based on the client’s current trust level.
