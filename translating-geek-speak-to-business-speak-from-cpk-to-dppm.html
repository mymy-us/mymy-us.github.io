Translating "Geek Speak" to Business Speak: From Cpk to DPPM

By Tim Y |Posted on September 10, 2025 |

Topic: Process Capability & Risk Assessment
The Academic View: Calculate $C_{pk}$. If the result is $>1.33$, the process is capable.
The Reality: Your boss and the Finance team do not care about the number “1.33.” They care about Risk and Scrap Costs.

You need to stop reporting just the index number and start translating it into DPPM (Defects Per Million).

The Logic: Why Convert?

In a boardroom, nobody asks, “What is the standard deviation within the subgroup?” They ask: “How many bad parts are we going to ship?”

According to the ASQ (American Society for Quality) and standard Six Sigma textbooks, there is a direct mathematical link between your Process Capability ($C_{pk}$) and the probability of a defect.

  • $C_{pk}$ = How well your process fits inside the goal posts (Specification Limits).
  • Sigma Level = The statistical robustness of the process.
  • DPPM = The predicted number of failures per million parts produced.

The Cheat Sheet (Short Term Capability)

Based on standard statistical tables (assuming a centered, normal distribution), here is the translation you need to memorize.

Cpk ValueProcess SigmaDPPM (Defects Per Million)The Reality
0.672.0 $\sigma$45,500Disaster. You are scrapping ~4.5% of your product. You are losing money on every run.
1.003.0 $\sigma$2,700The Danger Zone. 0.27% defect rate. In the automotive industry, this is often unacceptable.
1.334.0 $\sigma$63The Industry Standard. This is why 1.33 is the “magic number.” You drop from thousands of defects to just 63.
1.675.0 $\sigma$0.57World Class. Less than 1 defect per million.
2.006.0 $\sigma$0.002Six Sigma. Perfection.

How to Use This in a Meeting

Don’t just say: “Our Cpk is 1.0.” Say: “Our Cpk is 1.0. This predicts we will produce 2,700 defects per million parts. At our current volume, that is X bad parts per week. We need to improve the process to 1.33 to reduce that risk to near zero.”

A Warning on Sample Size

Garbage In, Garbage Out. You cannot calculate a real DPPM from 5 parts.

  • Academic Math: You can calculate Standard Deviation with $n=2$.
  • Business Reality: To trust the DPPM prediction, you need a significant sample size (usually 30 to 50 parts minimum).

If you calculate a $C_{pk}$ of 1.67 based on 5 parts, you are lying to yourself. The sample is too small to predict the long-term behavior of the process.

The Takeaway

$C_{pk}$ is not just a badge of honor for the PPAP file. It is a Financial Prediction.

  • Low $C_{pk}$ = High Defects = High Cost.
  • High $C_{pk}$ = Low Defects = Low Cost.

Learn to speak the language of Defects (DPPM), and management will listen to your engineering proposals.
