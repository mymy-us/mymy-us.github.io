Industry 4.0 is a Trap. Meet Squirrel 4.0.

By Tim Y |Posted on Sep 13, 2025 | 2 min read

Topic: Automation Strategy vs. Reality
The Academic View: Industry 4.0 is the future. “Lights Out” factories with AI and robots will replace human labor and solve all quality issues.
The Reality: If you automate a mess, you just get a faster and more expensive mess.

I learned this from watching them trying to automate the Barbie line.

A Million-Dollar Lesson

At the time, the company was aggressively pushing Industry 4.0 ideology. The goal was to cut human labor to cut costs. They installed complex, fully automated production lines from a 3rd party company to assemble the dolls.

These expensive robots trying to do the job was a catastrophe.

  1. Low Flexibility: Barbie fashions change every month. Humans can switch from Beach Barbie to Astronaut Barbie in seconds. However, the robots needed expensive reprogramming for every dress change.
  2. Frequent Downtime: When a human drops a doll, they pick it up and discard it. When a robot drops a doll, it jams the machine, stops the line, and requires a technician to fix it and a whole set of reset procedure.
  3. Cost: The depreciation and maintenance of the robots cost more than the labor they replaced.

Eventually, they had to scrap the automation plans and brought the humans back.

Enter Squirrel 4.0

The engineers and managers realized they couldn’t leap from manual to full auto without bridging. So the board launched a project playfully named: Squirrel 4.0.

The Philosophy: Just as a squirrel gathers nuts one by one to prepare for winter, engineers gather small, standardized improvements one by one (actually with a monthly quota, lmao) to prepare for automation.The ideology changes to: you cannot automate what you cannot stabilize.

How It Works

Instead of buying expensive robots, the team focused on LCIA (Low Cost Intelligent Automation).

Phase 1: The Human Sensor They kept the operators but standardized their movements strictly. They identified exactly where the pain points were.

  • The “Nut” includes every small kaizen, every standardized work instruction, every error-proofing device.

Phase 2: Partial Automation They didn’t replace the operator; they gave them better tools.

  • Example: Instead of a robot arm placing a screw, they installed a simple gravity-fed chute (costing around \$20) that delivers the screw to the operator’s handside without moving his head.
  • Result: Cycle time dropped, consistency went up, but the human flexibility remained.

Phase 3: Full Automation Only after the process was 100% stable—as they had stored enough nuts—did they look at robots again. By then, they knew exactly what the machine needed to be because the humans had already perfected the motion.
