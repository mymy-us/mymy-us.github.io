Building a Production Line from Scratch

By Tim Y |Posted on Sep 25, 2025 | 3 min read

Topic: New Product Introduction (NPI) & Line Setup
The Academic View: You are given a list of tasks with perfect cycle times, and you draw a perfectly balanced U-shaped cell workstation on paper.
The Reality: You are given only a 3D model (and sometime your computer’s crappy 3D viewer exhausts you, because the company only spends money on good high-end software licensing only for the design team), half the parts are missing, the cycle times are guesses, the floor has occupied by existed products and production starts in three months.

If you are a new Industrial Engineer assigned to an NPI team, setting up the physical line can be overwhelming. Here is a roadmap for how a line actually comes to life, and the paperwork you need to build it.

Phase 1: The Document Cascade

You cannot draw a physical floor plan until you know what you are building. In NPI, documents (we will only discuss documents related to industrial engineering at this time) are generated in a strict sequence. One feeds the next.

  1. The BOM (Bill of Materials): Tells you what parts you need.
  2. The Process Flow Diagram (PFD): Based on the BOM, you map out the steps you need to take. (e.g., Step 1: Screw base. Step 2: Attach wire. Step 3: Test).
  3. The PFMEA & Control Plan: Quality reviews the PFD to decide where things might fail. This tells you exactly where you must place inspection and testing stations on the physical line.
  4. The Time Study: You estimate or measure how long each step takes.
  5. The Line Balance: You group the steps together to meet the customer’s Takt Time. This tells you how many operators and workstations you need.
  6. The Physical Line Layout.

Rule of Thumb: Never start drawing the physical layout in AutoCAD until the Process Flow and Line Balance are drafted.

Phase 2: The Evolution of a Line

You do not build the final mass-production line on Day 1. The layout scales with the product’s maturity phases:

  • Prototype Stage: No line is needed. Engineers are just building the product on a workbench, or even on his own desk, to see if it turns on, that is the only purpose.
  • EVT / DVT (Engineering & Design Validation): You build a temporary line. It will be ugly and messy. You use folding tables, rolling carts, and PVC pipes in the lab. You are simply proving out the assembly sequence and figuring out what tools are required.
  • PVT (Production Validation): This is the pilot line. You lock down the space in the real jobshop and physical layout, assign operators to specific seats, and run at production speed to prove your cycle times.
  • Mass Production (SOP): The line is fixed, and your job shifts from “Setup” to continuous improvement.

Phase 3: Balancing Perfection

The biggest mistake young engineers make is trying to design a 100% perfect line before a single part is mass-produced.

During EVT and PVT, the product design will change. An operator will complain that a tool is too heavy. A cycle time you estimated at 10 seconds will actually take 30.

  • Do not bolt the tables to the floor.
  • Design the initial layout to be 80% good then it is enough.
  • Put your workstations and material racks on caster wheels.
  • Leave empty space between stations so you can add buffer inventory or extra test equipment later when things go wrong.
