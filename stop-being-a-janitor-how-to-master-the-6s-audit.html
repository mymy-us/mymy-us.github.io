Stop Being a Janitor: How to Master the 6S Audit

By Tim Y |Posted on September 16, 2025 |

Topic: 5S Implementation & Auditing
The Academic View: 5S (Sort, Set in Order, Shine, Standardize, Sustain) + Safety is about organizing the workplace.
The Reality: Most new engineers treat 5S audits like a cleaning inspection. You walk around, check a box if the floor is swept, and leave. This is useless.

A clean factory can still be a dangerous, inefficient factory. As a novice, you need to stop looking at the dirt and start looking for the “Band-Aids.”

Here is your methodology for a real 6S Audit.

Level 1: The “Don’t Die” Check (Safety)

Before you look for organization, look for immediate threats.

  • The Fire Trap: Is there cardboard or wood sitting next to a heat source (drying oven, welding bot)? This is the #1 audit finding for new grads.
  • The Trip Wire: Are air hoses or extension cords running across the walkway?
  • The “Jury Rig”: Look for duct tape. If you see duct tape holding a guard or a sensor in place, that is a safety violation disguised as a “temporary fix.”

Level 2: The “Band-Aid” Hunt (Shine = Inspection)

This is where you separate the novices from the pros. A novice sees a crumpled plastic bag stuffed into a gap in the machine and thinks: “Trash. 5S violation.” A pro sees the bag and thinks: “Why is that there?”

  • The Leak: Is the bag/rag there to catch an oil leak?
  • The Vibration: Is it wedged there to stop a panel from rattling?
  • The Sensor: Is it blocking a sensor to force the machine to run?

The Rule: If you see trash inside or on a machine, it is usually hiding a mechanical failure. Cleaning is Inspection. Don’t just pick it up; find the leak.

Level 3: The “Struggle” Check (Set in Order)

Stand at the station and watch the operator for 5 cycles. Do not look at the papers; look at their hands.

  • The Search: Do they have to turn around, walk away, or dig through a bin to find a wrench?
  • The Reach: Are they reaching over a moving part to grab a bolt?
  • The Reset: When they are done, do they have a specific place to put the tool back (Shadow Board), or do they just set it on the table?

If the operator has to think about where to put the tool, you failed “Set in Order.”

Level 4: The “Ghost” Check (Standardize)

Look at the Shadow Board or the Tool Outline.

  • Empty Shadows: If there is an outline for a hammer, but no hammer, where is it? Is the operator using a heavy wrench instead because the hammer is missing?
  • Ghost Tools: Are there tools on the desk that don’t have a home? This means the process changed, but the 5S didn’t keep up.

The Takeaway

Don’t walk around with a clipboard looking for dust bunnies. You are not a maid. You are a Detective.

  1. Find the heat sources.
  2. Find the duct tape and oil rags.
  3. Watch the operator’s hands for struggle.

If you fix the root cause of the mess, the mess goes away forever. If you just tell them to “clean it up,” it will be back tomorrow.
