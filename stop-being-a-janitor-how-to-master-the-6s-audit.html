    Stop Being a Janitor: How to Master the 6S Audit

    By Tim Y |Posted on Sep 16, 2025 | 3 min read

    Topic: 5S Implementation & Auditing
    The Academic View: 5S (Sort, Set in Order, Shine, Standardize, Sustain) + Safety is about organizing the workplace.
    The Reality: Most new engineers treat 5S audits like a cleaning inspection. And beside that, they are not familiar to the process and procedures. They walk around, check a box if the floor is swept, and leave. This does nothing.

    A clean factory can still be a dangerous, inefficient factory. As a novice, you need to stop looking at the dirt and start looking for the band-aids.

    Here is a methodology for a real 6S audit in a more head-friendly way.

    Level 1: The Most Fundamental Safety Check

    Before you look for organization, look for immediate threats.

    • The Fire Trap: Is there cardboard or wood sitting next to a heat source (drying oven, welding bot)?
    • The Trip Wire: Are air hoses or extension cords running across the walkway?
    • The “Jury Rig”: Look for duct tape. For example, if you see duct tape holding a guard or a sensor in place, that is a safety violation disguised as a “temporary fix.”
    • Etc.

    Level 2: The Band-Aid Hunt

    This is where people separate the novices from the pros. A novice sees a crumpled plastic bag stuffed into a gap in the machine and thinks: “Trash. 5S violation.” A pro sees the crumpled bag and thinks: “Why is that there?”

    • The Leak: Is the bag/rag there to catch an oil leak?
    • The Vibration: Is it wedged there to stop a panel from rattling?
    • The Sensor: Is it blocking a sensor to force the machine to run?

    The Rule: If you see trash inside or on a machine, it is usually hiding a mechanical failure. Cleaning is also inspection. Think beyond just picking it up; find the leak.

    Level 3: The Struggle Check

    Stand at the station and watch the operator does his job for a few cycles. Do not look at the SOP papers first; look at their hands.

    • The Search: Do they have to turn around, walk away, or dig through a bin to find a wrench?
    • The Reach: Are they reaching over a moving part to grab a bolt?
    • The Reset: When they are done, do they have a specific place to put the tool back, or do they just set it on the table?

    If the operator has to think about how to start and finish a motion, it failed the set-in-order check.

    Level 4: The Ghost Check

    Look at the shadow board or the tool outline.

    • Empty Shadows: If there is an outline for a hammer, but no hammer, where is it? Is the operator using a heavy wrench instead because the hammer is missing?
    • Ghost Tools: Are there tools on the desk that don’t have a place for reset? This means the process changed, or negelected, but the 5S didn’t keep up.

    The Takeaway

    Don’t walk around with a clipboard looking for dust bunnies. You are not a maid. You are hired to be an engineer, a detective! If you fix the root cause of an issue, the mess would be gone forever. If you just tell the line leader to clean it up, things will be back tomorrow.
