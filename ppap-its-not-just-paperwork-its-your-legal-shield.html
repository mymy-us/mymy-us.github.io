PPAP: It’s Not Just Paperwork, It’s Your Legal Shield

By Tim Y |Posted on September 7, 2025 | 2 min read

Topic: The True Purpose of PPAP (Production Part Approval Process)
The Academic View: PPAP demonstrates that a supplier can meet manufacturing requirements at the quoted production rate.
The Reality: PPAP is a Legal Contract. It is the “Constitution” of your product.

Here is why you need to treat it like a weapon, not a checklist.

1. The Shield: Protecting Your Company

Once the customer signs the PSW (Part Submission Warrant), the rules are frozen.

The Scenario:
You are six months into mass production. The customer complains that the surface texture is “too rough” or the color is “slightly off.”

The Defense:
You pull out the approved PPAP and the “Master Sample” (the physical part they signed).

  • If your current parts match the Master Sample: You are safe. The customer agreed to this standard. If they want to change it now, they must pay for it.
  • If you don’t have a signed PPAP: The customer can change their mind daily, and you have to pay for the rework.

Key Lesson: The signed PSW protects you from the customer’s changing opinions.

2. The Trap: The “Cycle Time” Lie

This is where Lean Engineers get burned.
In the PPAP, you document your Cycle Time (e.g., 60 seconds per part).

  • Do not cheat here.
  • Do not run the machine slow just to get good parts for the sample.

If you PPAP at 60 seconds but run Mass Production at 45 seconds to make more money, you have committed a Process Violation. If quality issues appear later, the customer will void your approval because you are running a different process than the one they approved.

3. The Warning: Unapproved Changes

This is the “Third Rail”—touch it and you die.

If you change anything in the process after the PPAP is signed, you must tell the customer.

  • Changing a cutting tool brand? Notify them.
  • Moving the machine to a different spot on the floor? Notify them.
  • Reducing inspection frequency? Notify them.

If you make a change without approval (even if it makes the part better), and a recall happens:

  1. You are liable. The lawyers will see you didn’t follow the PPAP.
  2. You lose your certification (IATF 16949).
  3. Your company faces massive fines.

The Takeaway

Don’t copy-paste your PPAP documents just to get it done.

  • Be Honest: Only promise a cycle time and tolerance you can actually hit every day.
  • Be Strict: Once it is signed, the factory floor must follow it exactly.

Treat the PPAP like a court testimony. Because one day, it might be the only thing standing between you and a million-dollar fine.
